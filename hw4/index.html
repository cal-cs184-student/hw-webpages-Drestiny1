Part 1: Masses and springs

We model cloth as a serious of point masses connected by springs. Each point mass is connected to neighboring points using three constraints: structural, shearing, and bending. Structural constraints connects each mass to its immediate horizontal and vertical neighbors creating a grid shape. Shearing connects diagonal neighbors preventing cloth from collapsing along the diagonals, which results in a more stable structure. Bending constraints connects masses two units apart, helping smooth and allowing for resistance against sharp folds.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

The spring constant ks controls the stiffness of the cloth. Low ks stretches more, sags more, or looks more loose. High ks means a stiffer cloth, which resists stretching.
Density affects the mass of each point in the cloth. Low density results in a lighter cloth and less sagging. High density results in a heavier cloth and more sagging.
Damping controls how quickly the cloth loses energy. Low damping results in more bounces and oscillations before resting. High damping means it reaches the resting point more quickly and the cloth settles more smoothly. As you can see in the images below, which is captured mid flight, with low damping, the cloth tilts back and forth, whereas for high damping, the cloth just slowly moves down until it gets to a vertical position.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

I implemented collision handling so that the cloth interacts correctly with spheres and planes. How it works is that it detects whether a point mass moves inside or through a surface and if it does, it adjusts the position so that it stays outside the surface.

For the spheres, I check whether a point mass is inside the sphere by comparing its distance from the sphere's orgin to the radius. If it is inside, I compute where the sphere should have intersected the sphere, getting the tangent point. Then I compute a correction vector and scale it by (1-friction) to simulate energy loss.

For plane collisions, I check whether the point mass has crossed to the other side of the plane. If it has, I compute the intersection / tangent point. Then I move the point mass by a small offset so that it remains on the correct side of the plane. I also apply a correction and scale by (1-friction).

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

In this part, I implemented self collisions, which is when the cloth collides with itself. To do this, I implemented spatial hashing to reduce the number of collision checks needed. Instead of checking each point with every other point mass, I partition the space into 3D box volumes and only check for collision within the boxes.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program controls how objects are drawn, such as by determining an position, color, lighting, and texture appearances.

Vertex shaders run on each vertex in a mesh. They determine position and where a vetex should appear next.

Fragment shaders are per pixel and this is where lighting is computed, textures are applied, and the final color is decided.

The Blinn-Phong Shading Model approximates how light interacts with a surface by breaking it into three components: ambient, diffuse, and specular.

Ambient represents indirect lighting scattered throughout the scene and it's constant, affecting all surfaces equally. Objects will appear flat or unlit with this component.

Diffuse represents how light reflects off rough surfaces. This depends on the angle between the surfaces and the direction of the light. Some surfaces will appear lighter, while others darker, allowing the object's shape to take form.

Specular represents bright highlights that appear on shiny surfaces. When rendering only this component, objects will mostly appear dark except for bright highlights.

When combined, these three components allow for more realistic rendering.

Both Bump and Displacement mapping use texture data to simulate surface textures. Bump mapping modfies surface normals during shading to create an illusion of texture details without changing the actual geometry, so the surface appears textured, but the silhouette of the object remains the same. Displacement mapping, on the hand, physically alters the geometry.

When comparing different mesh resolutions, low mesh resolution displacement mapping produces more blocky chunks and deformations due to limited vertices. Bump mapping still appears smooth though because it doesn't depend on mesh resolution.
